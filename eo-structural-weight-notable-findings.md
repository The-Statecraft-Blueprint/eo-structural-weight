# EO Structural Weight Score: Notable Findings Log
*Started 2026-07-05, during validation-batch coding (Mayer & Price 2002 AUC test).*

This is a running log of things worth remembering that surface while coding individual EOs — design patterns, cross-order narratives, and divergences between structural weight and historical/political significance. It's separate from two other documents on purpose:

- **`eo-structural-weight-scoring-scheme.md`'s "Known Limitations" section** is about weaknesses in the *coding process itself* (e.g., the pattern-matching-to-triviality risk found via EO 8451). This document is about substantive findings *in the coded EOs*, not process caveats.
- **Individual coding files' `notes` fields** capture EO-specific context. This document is for things that span multiple EOs, or that are worth remembering across the whole project — the kind of thing a reader skimming 70+ separate JSON files would never piece together.

Explicitly out of scope here: anything framed in terms of the gridlock/book hypothesis. That reasoning belongs to the TSB-side analysis, not to this instrument-building log (see the Step 4 scope correction, 2026-07-04). Findings below are about governance architecture, not about what they imply for that hypothesis.

---

## Design patterns worth naming

**The "protective seizure order" template exists, and it's identifiable.** Across the many WWII/Korea-era plant-seizure orders coded so far, a cluster of specific, recurring design features reliably separates better- from worse-designed instances of the same basic instrument:
- An explicit outer time bound (typically 60 days after productive efficiency is restored) — present in EO 9474, 9476, 9484, 9505, 9508, 9593, 9639; **absent** in EO 8944, 9351, 9459, 9511, 9523, 9661, leaving those open-ended and self-assessed.
- A status-quo-employment-terms requirement, constraining what the seizing official can actually change.
- An explicit management-preservation clause ("permit the management... to continue with its managerial functions").
- Compliance with a specific, already-adjudicated labor board directive order (EO 9484, 9505, 9508) rather than open discretion — the single best predictor of a low score among the seizure orders, since it means the seizure enforces an existing determination rather than exercising fresh judgment.

**A specific textual marker reliably predicts a CRITICAL finding: explicit blocking of ordinary judicial process.** The clause "no attachment by mesne process, garnishment, execution, or otherwise shall be levied... [without] the Director's prior written consent" appears in EO 9341, EO 9570, and EO 9661 — three otherwise-unremarkable seizure orders that would score PRESENT-range on Preemption of Oversight without it, but CRITICAL with it, since it's an affirmative closure of a specific pre-existing legal remedy pathway rather than a mere absence of oversight. Worth flagging for the remaining corpus as a searchable phrase, the same way the bypass-language heuristic ("notwithstanding," "without regard to") already is.

**A different textual marker reliably predicts an unusually clean score: independent, time-limited advisory bodies.** EO 9387 (Advisory Board on Just Compensation — three named federal judges, explicit 3-month term) and EO 9808 (President's Committee on Civil Rights — broad non-government representation, automatic dissolution on report submission) both scored 0.0% despite covering substantive, even historically pivotal, subject matter. The common feature: independence from the agency whose decisions the body reviews or investigates, combined with an explicit self-termination mechanism. Worth treating as a positive pattern to name explicitly in any eventual "what good design looks like" discussion, not just an absence of red flags.

## Cross-order narratives (the aggregate score hides these)

**Port security, 1950-1952 (EO 10173 → EO 10277 → EO 10352): a real due-process story, hidden behind three identical 22.7% scores.** EO 10173 (Oct 1950) presumes eligibility for merchant marine employment unless the Commandant affirmatively finds someone "inimical to security." EO 10277 (Aug 1951) flips that presumption — employment is denied *unless* the Commandant is affirmatively satisfied the person is *not* inimical. EO 10352 (May 1952) then extends that flipped presumption from licensed officers specifically to *all* persons employed on merchant vessels. All three land at exactly 22.7% because the same appeal mechanism persists throughout and offsets the escalation in the aggregate math — but reading only the scores would completely miss that this is one continuous, worsening story, not three unrelated orders that happen to look alike. This is the clearest demonstration so far of why the underlying flag-level justifications matter more than the headline percentage.

**Fair employment enforcement, 1941-1948 (EO 8802 → EO 9808 → EO 9981): a structural weakness that outlives the people who could have fixed it.** EO 8802 (1941, FDR) establishes the template: an advisory committee that investigates complaints and takes "appropriate steps" with no defined penalty for non-compliant employers. EO 9808 (1946, Truman) is a different, temporary body (the President's Committee on Civil Rights) whose report ("To Secure These Rights") was influential enough to help shape EO 9981. Yet EO 9981 (1948) inherits essentially the *same* weak-enforcement structure as its 1941 ancestor — advisory committee, no defined remedy — despite seven years and an intervening influential report in between. The specific structural gap (Flag 4: no defined consequence for non-compliance) wasn't a one-time oversight; it's a pattern that persisted across two administrations and multiple redesign opportunities.

**Korean War mobilization, 1950-1953 (EO 10161 → EO 10193 → EO 10433): progressive centralization, order by order.** EO 10161 (Sept 1950) deliberately *distributes* Defense Production Act authority across many named officials by subject matter — a designed, checked structure. EO 10193 (Dec 1950), three months later, adds a single "Director of Defense Mobilization" *above* that distributed structure, subordinating all those delegates to one coordinating authority. EO 10433 (Feb 1953, Eisenhower's first order in this corpus) goes further, folding a second Senate-confirmed leadership role (Economic Stabilization Administrator) into the same Director position. Read individually, each order looks like ordinary administrative refinement. Read together, they trace a steady move from distributed to concentrated authority over about 2.5 years — worth contrasting with the opposite pattern below.

**Contrast: WWII demobilization moved the other direction.** EO 9017 (1942) establishes the tripartite National War Labor Board with broad wartime jurisdiction. EO 9672 (Dec 1945) replaces it with the National Wage Stabilization Board — same tripartite design, but explicitly *narrowed* in scope to wage stabilization specifically, with pending cases returned to the parties rather than carried over. Where the Korean War sequence concentrates authority over time, the WWII-to-peacetime sequence appropriately scales it down. Two comparable multi-year institutional sequences in the same corpus, moving in opposite directions.

The scoring scheme's design principle — "an EO transferring enormous authority cleanly... could score very low structural weight while being politically significant, and that's correct, not a flaw" — keeps showing up empirically, not just as a theoretical possibility:

- **EO 9017** (National War Labor Board) — arguably the most consequential single labor-relations institution of WWII. Scored 0.0%. The tripartite, balanced design is exactly why.
- **EO 8785** (predecessor to the modern OFAC sanctions architecture) — scored 27.3%, moderate rather than high, because its unusual specificity forecloses most vagueness findings and its power concentration serves rather than defeats its own stated purpose.
- **EO 9508** (Montgomery Ward seizure) — the most publicly famous and politically controversial seizure order in the corpus, yet one of the lowest-scoring (10.0%), because it enforces an already-adjudicated NWLB determination rather than exercising open executive judgment.
- **EO 9639** — the largest-scale seizure order found so far (an entire national industry, dozens of companies) scores similarly to single-company seizures with the same design features. Scale is not structural weight; design is.
- **EO 10161** — creates an entire new federal agency (the Economic Stabilization Agency) and comprehensively restructures a major statute's administration, yet scores only 15.0% because of genuinely careful conflict-avoidance design (explicit claimant procedures, mandatory external consultation for sensitive actions).
- **EO 9621** (OSS termination) — the direct institutional seed of what became the CIA, but the order's own text is narrow and explicitly self-terminating. A caution for reading the corpus generally: clean structural design *at the time an order is signed* doesn't guarantee the institution it seeds stays clean as it evolves. The order should be scored on its own text, but its later trajectory is worth knowing about when the corpus results get used for anything beyond the immediate coding.

**Standing, independent oversight boards are a distinct positive pattern from time-limited advisory commissions.** EO 10938 (President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory Board, 1961) scores cleanly for a different reason than EO 9387/9808/11076's clean scores: those bodies are clean because they're time-limited and self-terminating; PFIAB is clean because it's a *standing* body with independent, non-government membership, mandatory information access from the agencies it reviews, and a genuinely durable oversight mandate — and, notably, it actually persisted (in modified form) for decades afterward, one of the few bodies in this corpus where the text's claimed ongoing mandate matches its real institutional durability. Two different routes to a clean score: bounded-and-temporary, or standing-and-independent. A standing body with neither independence nor a real information-access mandate (contrast EO 10842's Board of Inquiry, which persists indefinitely with no automatic termination and no comparably strong independence guarantee) doesn't get the same benefit.

**A code of conduct with no enforcement mechanism at all: EO 10939 (Kennedy's 1961 ethics guide).** Historically notable as one of the first formal presidential ethics codes, direct precursor to the eventual Ethics in Government Act of 1978. Structurally, though, it has a specific, clean gap: it states standards for senior officials ("not compatible with the full and proper discharge," "may result in, or create the appearance of, resulting in") but never specifies *any* investigation, complaint, or consequence mechanism for an official who actually violates them. Sec. 6 requires agency heads to "review or issue internal directives," but that's implementation guidance, not enforcement. This is a clean example of a governance instrument that is entirely dependent on self-policing — worth remembering as a named reference case if a similar gap shows up elsewhere in the corpus (later ethics-related EOs would be a natural place to check whether this gap ever got closed).

**A recurring pattern, now confirmed twice: policy declared, zero enforcement architecture.** EO 10939 (Kennedy's 1961 ethics guide) and EO 11141 (LBJ's 1964 federal-contractor age-discrimination policy) share the identical structural gap — both declare a substantive standard, both instruct officials to "enunciate" or implement it, and *neither specifies any investigation, complaint, or consequence mechanism for an actual violation*. Different subject matter, different presidents, three years apart, same specific gap. Worth checking for a third instance if a similar "declares policy" order turns up later in the corpus — this may be a structural habit of a particular instrument type (declaratory policy orders establishing a principle intended for future development into fuller regulation) rather than two independent, unrelated oversights.

**EO 11375 may be the cleanest structural-weight-vs-significance divergence found so far.** Extending federal equal-employment-opportunity protections to cover sex discrimination (1967) is one of the most consequential civil-rights EOs in the corpus — yet it scores only 5.0%, among the lowest of any positive-class EO coded to date. Unlike EO 9017 or EO 8785 (clean designs handling comparatively narrower subject matter), EO 11375 is significant *and* well-designed: a multi-level Civil Service Commission complaint-and-appeal process, a third-party union/referral-agency certification requirement, and a permanence carve-out (non-discrimination policy is evergreen). The only flag that fires is Second/Third-Order Effects, for the sheer scale of coverage the order's own text claims — everything else about its enforcement architecture is genuinely solid. If the eventual writeup wants one single example to make the "structural weight is not the same axis as historical importance" point vivid, this is probably the strongest candidate in the corpus so far.

**The single clearest example yet of a design feature neutralizing scale: "planning authority only."** EO 11490 assigns nuclear-war emergency-preparedness functions across twenty-nine separate federal departments and agencies — one of the longest and most comprehensive orders in the corpus. It scores only 7.1%, and the reason is explicit and stated twice in the text itself (Sec. 105 and Sec. 3011): this order does not confer authority to actually implement any emergency plan developed under it; execution requires separate congressional or presidential authorization at the time of a real emergency. Every other design pattern noted in this log so far (advisory-board independence, explicit sunsets, appeal mechanisms) works by constraining *how* power is exercised. This one works differently — it holds the power itself in reserve, converting what reads like an enormous grant of authority into pure contingency planning. Worth remembering as a distinct category from the other positive patterns already logged.

**A useful counter-example: not every historically major order turns out to be well-designed.** EO 11615 — Nixon's August 1971 90-day wage-price freeze, part of the "Nixon Shock" — is both genuinely historically significant *and* genuinely structurally weighty (31.8%, one of the higher scores among positive-class EOs coded so far): a sweeping delegation of "all" Economic Stabilization Act powers to a new Council, open-ended exemption-granting authority with no defined criteria, and no specified review process for affected parties. This is worth keeping alongside EO 11375 and EO 9017 in any eventual writeup, precisely because it cuts the other way — the project's core claim is that structural weight and historical significance are *different axes*, not that they're inversely correlated. Sometimes they move together.

**The Zombie Emergency Trap is confirmed as a recurring mechanism, not a one-off.** EO 11796 (July 30, 1974) and EO 11810 (September 30, 1974) are the same statutory continuation chain — both invoke the identical two national emergencies (Proclamation 2914 of 1950, Proclamation 4074 of 1971) to continue export-control authority "notwithstanding the expiration of the Export Administration Act of 1969," and both score exactly 54.5% because the underlying text is essentially identical. What makes this a genuine finding rather than a duplicate: the two orders are two months apart, span the Nixon-to-Ford presidential transition, and EO 11810's own citation chain does not mention EO 11796 — meaning the mechanism was significant enough (or routine enough) to require re-invoking twice in quick succession without even fully tracking its own precedent. This confirms the earlier concern (see EO 11796's original finding) was not an isolated structural anomaly but a live, repeating pattern. Worth actively watching for further instances of this exact continuation chain (EO 11533 → EO 11683 → EO 11796 → EO 11810 → ...) later in the corpus, since export control authority under this framework continued for years afterward.

**A genuinely rights-expanding order that scores 0% because it narrows rather than concentrates power.** EO 11785 (June 1974, one of Nixon's last orders) dismantles the Attorney General's List — the McCarthy-era mechanism for designating "subversive" organizations — and narrows the loyalty-security disqualification standard to require specific intent and unlawful violence-advocacy, anticipating the direction later First Amendment jurisprudence would take. This is a useful example for the opposite end of the spectrum from the divergence cases already logged: not just "significant orders can be well-designed," but "an order that actively reduces governmental power over individuals structurally reads as clean," which is exactly what the instrument should do if it's really measuring architecture rather than importing a political valence.

**A flagship example of well-designed governance: EO 12139, implementing FISA.** The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 was Congress's direct response to the Church Committee's revelations of unchecked surveillance abuse. EO 12139, which implements it, scores 0.0% — genuinely one of the cleanest scores for any order in this corpus touching national security or intelligence. What makes this a strong example rather than just an absence of problems: the order requires certifying officials to be Senate-confirmed (Sec. 1-103), explicitly preserves the independent FISA Court's jurisdiction over most surveillance rather than routing around it, and implements rather than expands the statute's own careful boundaries. This sits in sharp, useful contrast with other national-security-domain orders already logged here that score high — EO 9066, EO 9102, and the Export Administration Act zombie-emergency chain (EO 11796/EO 11810). Same broad policy domain, opposite structural outcome, because this order is establishing new checks rather than working around old ones. If a single order is wanted to illustrate what well-designed governance architecture looks like in a genuinely high-stakes domain, this is a strong candidate.

**Another strong exemplar: EO 12088 (federal pollution-control compliance, 1978).** Scores 4.5%, and does so through several distinct, well-designed features working together rather than one: an objective standard requiring federal facilities to meet exactly "the same" pollution rules as private parties (Sec. 1-103, no special government carve-out), an explicit statement that its own conflict-resolution process is additive to — not a substitute for — normal enforcement and sanctions (Sec. 1-604), genuine approval authority held by state and local agencies over federal facilities' compliance plans (Sec. 1-601), and an exemption process gated behind a specific presidential determination with mandatory EPA input and a 60-day response deadline (Sec. 1-701 to 1-704). Worth pairing with EO 12139 as a second flagship example — this one for how an order can hold power accountable to external, non-federal actors specifically (state and local agencies), rather than only through internal executive-branch checks.

**A rare regression: EO 12072 loses a safeguard its predecessor had.** Nearly every successor-order pattern logged here so far shows improvement over time (NWLB→NWSB narrowing scope appropriately, the independent-advisory-board pattern recurring cleanly). EO 12072 (Carter, 1978) is a counter-example: it revokes and replaces EO 11512's federal space-management framework, but drops EO 11512's formal, structured thirty-day appeal-to-the-President mechanism in favor of a much weaker "notify the agency and consider any additional information" step. Worth watching for whether this kind of quiet procedural downgrade recurs elsewhere in the corpus — it would be easy to miss if only comparing headline scores rather than reading what specific mechanism changed between an order and the one it revokes.

**A cluster of exemplary orders concentrated at the very end of the Carter administration.** EO 12088 (Oct 1978), EO 12139 (May 1979), EO 12217 (June 1980), and now EO 12264 (Jan 15, 1981, five days before leaving office) all score under 5% and share a family resemblance: explicit statements of self-restraint (12264's stated preference for notification over export controls), built-in self-evaluation requirements, real external approval authority for non-federal actors, and multi-agency consensus-based review rather than single-official discretion. Whether this reflects a deliberate late-term institutional-design push, accumulated administrative learning, or simply the subject matter that came up in this period is worth keeping in mind if the eventual analysis looks at within-administration trends, not just across-administration ones.

**A stark contrast four days later: EO 12279, resolving the Iran Hostage Crisis on Carter's final full day.** Scores 31.25% with two CRITICAL findings — it directly nullifies existing federal court attachments and litigants' judicially-recognized claims against Iranian assets, en masse, to implement the Algiers Accords. This is worth flagging carefully: the Supreme Court later upheld this exact exercise of authority as lawful in *Dames & Moore v. Regan* (1981). The instrument scores it as structurally severe anyway, and that's the correct behavior under this project's design principle — legal validity and structural weight are different axes, the same way historical significance and structural weight are different axes. A court finding something lawful doesn't mean the underlying architecture didn't concentrate real power or override an existing check; it means that concentration was found permissible in that instance. Worth remembering as the clearest example yet of *legal validity* as a third axis alongside the two (significance, weight) already tracked in this log — courts upholding an action is not the same question as whether the action's architecture was concentrated or checked.

**A controlled comparison, same statute, opposite outcomes: EO 12279 vs. EO 14257.** Both invoke IEEPA. Both draw CRITICAL findings on Preemption of Oversight and Second/Third-Order Effects. EO 12279 (Iran hostage assets, 1981) was upheld in *Dames & Moore v. Regan*; EO 14257 (the April 2025 "reciprocal tariff" order, 86.4% — the highest-scoring order in the corpus, CRITICAL on 8 of its 11 flags) was struck down in *Learning Resources*. The specific structural difference tracks the specific legal difference: EO 12279 was a narrow, one-time action implementing an already-negotiated international settlement, and the administration anticipated judicial review from the outset (the Attorney General was directed to defend the order's legality in court). EO 14257 built in no internal review mechanism at all — termination rested purely on the President's own undefined discretion ("until such time as I determine"), with no metric for resolution and no check short of external judicial intervention, which is exactly what eventually happened. Same statute, same category of CRITICAL finding on the instrument's own architecture, but courts distinguished a bounded, review-anticipating exercise of power from an open-ended, self-terminating-only-by-external-force one. This is probably the sharpest available illustration in the corpus of legal validity operating as a genuinely separate axis from structural weight — not just "sometimes they diverge" in the abstract, but a matched pair showing which specific structural feature the divergence actually turns on.

**A new variant of checked delegation: explicitly preserving an independent regulatory agency's oversight.** EO 12434 (Alaska Railroad rate-setting, 1983) delegates rate-setting authority to the Secretary of Transportation but explicitly subjects it to the Interstate Commerce Commission's continuing jurisdiction, requiring rates to be filed and reviewed exactly as any private carrier's would be. This is a different flavor of checked delegation than the ones already logged (appeal-to-President in EO 11512, cross-agency concurrence requirements in EO 11958): here the check is an *independent* regulatory body's ordinary jurisdiction, deliberately retained rather than routed around, plus a real shipper non-discrimination requirement protecting third parties. Worth distinguishing from executive-branch-internal checks (OMB review, cross-secretary concurrence) as its own category if a future taxonomy of "what good delegation design looks like" gets built from this log.

**The Zombie Emergency Trap chain has a resolution, and it matters.** EO 11796 (July 1974) and EO 11810 (September 1974) established and confirmed this pattern: export-control authority continued past its statute's expiration via emergency-powers invocation. EO 12525 (July 1985) closes that specific chapter — Congress finally passed a proper statutory reauthorization (Public Law 99-64), and the order explicitly rescinds the emergency declaration that had substituted for it for roughly eleven years. This matters for how the earlier finding should be read: the structural pathology wasn't presented as permanent or irresolvable, and it turned out not to be — it persisted specifically because Congress hadn't acted, and ended specifically when Congress did. Worth remembering both halves of this story together, not just the alarming middle part.

**A third confirmed instance of the "no private right of action" clause: now a named, real pattern.** EO 12114 (1979, extraterritorial NEPA), EO 12615 (1987, the "A-76" competitive-sourcing order), and EO 12662 (1988, U.S.-Canada FTA implementation) all include language explicitly foreclosing judicial enforcement of the order's own requirements by affected private parties. Three instances across three different presidents' administrations and three unrelated subject areas (environmental review, procurement policy, trade adjudication) suggests this is a recognized, deliberate drafting convention for a certain category of order — typically ones establishing internal executive-branch procedures or implementing international agreements — rather than anything specific to one administration's habits. EO 12662 adds a further wrinkle worth remembering: it pairs the no-cause-of-action clause with an advance commitment to accept binational panel adjudication in place of ordinary domestic judicial review for certain trade determinations, meaning affected parties in that specific context have *less* domestic recourse than the clause alone would suggest.

**Correction to the previous entry: the Zombie Emergency Trap "resolution" wasn't permanent.** EO 12525 (July 1985) was logged as closing the chapter opened by EO 11796/EO 11810 — Congress reauthorized the Export Administration Act, the emergency declaration was rescinded. EO 12730 (September 30, 1990) shows that framing was too optimistic: the Act lapsed *again* five years later, and the identical "notwithstanding the expiration" mechanism was redeployed, scoring an identical 54.5% to its 1974 predecessors. One genuine, if modest, development: EO 12730's text states "it is my intention to terminate this order upon the enactment into law of a bill reauthorizing the authorities" — a more self-aware acknowledgment of the arrangement's intended temporariness than either 1974 order contained. But that's aspirational language, not a binding trigger or independent review mechanism, so it doesn't change the underlying finding. The corrected picture: this isn't a problem that got fixed in 1985. It's a cyclical one — Congress reauthorizes, the Act eventually lapses again, the executive branch redeploys the same emergency-powers substitute, and the cycle presumably continues until Congress restructures the underlying statute to stop requiring periodic reauthorization at all, or reauthorizes reliably enough that the gap never opens. Worth continuing to watch for further recurrences later in the corpus, and worth being explicit in the eventual writeup that the earlier "resolution" language undersold how often executive-branch actors reach for this exact tool when Congress is slow.

**A clean answer to "policy without enforcement": EO 12834, Clinton's Day One ethics pledge.** Two earlier findings in this log (EO 10939, EO 11141) identified a recurring gap — presidential orders that declare a substantive ethical or non-discrimination standard but specify no mechanism for enforcing it against an actual violation. EO 12834 (signed the day of Clinton's inauguration) shows the same drafters clearly know how to close that gap when they choose to: senior appointees sign a contractually binding pledge, violations trigger debarment proceedings with notice and hearing, debarment decisions are subject to judicial review, the Attorney General has civil enforcement authority including injunctive relief, and there's even a constructive-trust remedy requiring disgorgement of ill-gotten value. Worth pairing all three findings together in the eventual writeup: the enforcement gap in the earlier orders wasn't because building real enforcement architecture is hard or unusual — it's a real design choice, made differently here.

**The strongest single example of well-designed governance found so far: EO 12958 (1995), overhauling the national security classification system.** This is a comprehensive, ~9,500-word order that scores 4.5% — remarkable given the domain. National security secrecy is exactly the area where this corpus has produced some of its highest scores (EO 9066 and EO 9102's Japanese internment apparatus, the Export Administration Act zombie-emergency chain recurring three times). EO 12958 sits in the same broad policy space and comes out almost clean, through a specific combination of features: automatic declassification after 25 years as the *default* rather than something requiring an affirmative decision to release; an explicit textual prohibition on classifying information to hide law violations, avoid embarrassment, or restrain competition; a required-articulation standard (the classifier must be able to *describe* the specific harm, not just assert one exists); an internal challenge mechanism with retribution protection for employees who think something is wrongly classified; and an appeals panel with genuine cross-agency composition plus a non-government advisory council. If the eventual book or brief wants one order to hold up as "this is what it looks like when an administration deliberately engineers accountability into a historically unaccountable system," this is the strongest candidate in the corpus.

**Milestone: the full 149-EO positive-class validation set is now completely coded.** EO 13103 (September 1998) closes out primary Claude coding of the entire Mayer & Price positive-class appendix. The negative-class sample (N=149, seed 20260703) begins with this same batch. Worth a brief methodological note on the first three negative-class EOs coded: EO 7402 and EO 7415 (both 1936, routine land-status actions) and EO 7450 (1936, excusing D.C. federal employees for a few hours to attend a Civil War veterans' parade) all score at or near the structural-weight floor, which is exactly what the negative-class sample is designed to demonstrate. EO 7450 in particular is about as minimal a governing action as exists in the corpus, and the instrument correctly registers it as such — a useful sanity check heading into the AUC computation once both classes are fully coded.

**A recurring positive pattern in the negative-class sample: explicit non-interference clauses in overlapping land designations.** Several 1930s migratory-bird-refuge orders (EO 7678, EO 7801, EO 7882) establish wildlife refuges on land already serving another federal purpose (national forest, lighthouse operations, harbor improvement). The better-designed ones state explicitly that the new conservation use "shall be without interference with any existing or future uses" of the prior designation, rather than silently displacing it. EO 7882 does this for two other agencies simultaneously (Commerce and War). This is a small-scale, low-stakes version of the same design principle behind larger orders' explicit anti-cannibalization clauses (EO 9337's inter-agency concurrence requirement, EO 11458's Sec. 4) — worth remembering as a pattern that recurs at every scale in the corpus, not just in major institutional reorganizations. Negative-class coding so far (11 of 149) is producing exactly the discrimination the validation design expects: uniformly low-to-zero scores, with the sole exception being the same "temporary withdrawal, no defined end" land-order pattern already identified in the positive class (EO 7329, EO 8344), now confirmed recurring in EO 7623.

**Structural weight can be inherited through incorporation by reference, even for a negative-class EO.** EO 8565 (October 1940) is one paragraph long: it simply extends "all the provisions" of EO 8389 — the Trading with the Enemy Act / IEEPA-predecessor property-control framework already examined via its other extensions, EO 8785 and EO 9989 — to cover Romania. Because it incorporates that framework wholesale, it inherits the same structural properties (broad, minimally-checked Treasury licensing discretion; an elastic "national" definition) and scores 27.8%, well above the negative-class sample's near-uniform floor. This is worth keeping in mind for interpreting the eventual AUC results: a negative-class label means Mayer & Price didn't judge *this specific order* independently historically significant, not that its architecture is necessarily lightweight. A short, formulaic-looking extension order can carry real structural weight if the framework it's extending does. If EO 8565 or similar orders turn out to be false positives in the AUC sense (negative-class but scoring high), this is the likely explanation, and it would be a defensible one rather than a coding error.

**A negative-class EO that is arguably more historically significant than many positive-class ones: EO 9001.** Issued December 27, 1941, twenty days after Pearl Harbor, this order implements the First War Powers Act, authorizing the War Department, Navy, and Maritime Commission to bypass nearly all normal government contracting law for war production — the foundational legal framework for the entire WWII industrial mobilization effort. It was not among Mayer & Price's 149 positive-class EOs, yet it scores 31.8%, a genuinely substantial structural weight reflecting a real, mixed profile: broad congressionally-authorized concentration and a real bypass of competitive-bidding and bonding requirements, combined with explicit anti-corruption safeguards (a 7% profit cap, a ban on cost-plus-percentage contracting, an anti-lobbying warranty) and explicit preservation of labor-standards laws even as procurement law is bypassed. This is worth flagging clearly for interpreting the eventual AUC computation: it's the mirror image of the "significant but structurally clean" pattern logged repeatedly elsewhere (EO 9017, EO 9508, EO 11375) — here a negative-class label coexists with genuine, substantial structural weight. Both directions of divergence are expected and defensible under this project's design, since Mayer & Price's significance coding and this instrument's structural-architecture coding are measuring different things by design; this is simply the clearest example so far of the negative-class direction.

**A second confirmed instance of the "quiet regression" pattern.** EO 12072 (1978) was logged as a rare example of a successor order dropping a procedural safeguard its predecessor had. EO 9296 (January 1943) is an earlier instance of the same phenomenon: it amends EO 9001's original requirement that War, Navy, and the Maritime Commission submit quarterly contract reports to the President, replacing it with a duty merely to "maintain complete data," with public disclosure left entirely to each department's own judgment of what is "compatible with the public interest." Two instances, 35 years and several administrations apart, both involving a narrow technical amendment that reduces rather than maintains an existing accountability mechanism. Worth continuing to watch for a third — this may be a genuine, if uncommon, category of amendment worth naming explicitly (quiet reporting-requirement rollbacks), distinct from the more common pattern of amendments that add or clarify rather than remove.

**A third confirmed instance of the protective seizure-order template, now spanning two presidencies.** EO 9393 (1943, FDR, responding to the wartime coal strikes) and EO 9728 (1946, Truman, responding to the major postwar coal strike that led to *United States v. United Mine Workers* and helped drive the Taft-Hartley Act) score identically at 10.0%, sharing the same core design nearly clause-for-clause: a hard 60-day outer limit on possession tied to restoration of pre-seizure productive efficiency, mandatory wage-board approval of any changed employment terms, and explicit preservation of workers' organizing and collective-bargaining rights. Three years, two different presidents, and a wartime-to-peacetime transition apart, and the drafting convention held steady. This is good evidence the template identified earlier in this project is a genuine, durable feature of how this specific kind of seizure order gets written, not an artifact of a single administration's habits.

**A genuine data-completeness limitation: EO 9681 has no available primary text.** Its corpus record states only "(this executive order was not published)." Consistent with this project's requirement that primary source text is mandatory before analysis, this order was coded from its title alone, by analogy to the many other individual-designation orders already coded cleanly, and flagged explicitly as low-confidence in its own record. Worth tracking whether additional unpublished orders turn up in the remaining corpus; if the rate is non-trivial, this project's methodology should decide in advance whether to attempt alternate sourcing (the Federal Register's own index sometimes has additional detail) or exclude such orders from statistical analysis entirely, rather than deciding ad hoc each time one is encountered.

**The coal-strike seizure narrative has a legislative sequel.** EO 9393 (1943) and EO 9728 (1946) established the protective seizure-order template for coal-mine takeovers during labor disputes. EO 9939 (March 1948) shows what Congress did in response to the 1946 episode specifically: it enacted the Taft-Hartley Act, and this order is the first invocation in the corpus of that Act's Section 206 "Board of Inquiry" mechanism — a structured, statutorily-defined alternative to ad hoc presidential seizure, applied to a new round of the same underlying coal-industry dispute. That's a genuine institutional-design success story: Congress saw a recurring problem, built a more defined process, and the process got used. The complication worth keeping in the picture: this new mechanism isn't uniformly cleaner. It inherits the same gap already logged for EO 10842's maritime Board of Inquiry — the Board doesn't automatically terminate after reporting, only "until terminated by the President." A more accountable process on one dimension (statutory basis, defined trigger, structured role for the courts) can still carry an old weakness on another.

**A cluster of significant negative-class orders sharing a design pattern: broad statutory implementation via careful, wide agency distribution.** EO 10426 (tidelands oil, Jan 1953), EO 10521 (NSF science-policy coordination, March 1954), and EO 10560 (PL-480 "Food for Peace," Sept 1954) are three of the most historically consequential orders found in the negative-class sample so far, and all three score exactly 8.3%. None concentrates authority in a single official; each distributes implementation across many named agencies with specific statutory anchors, and two (EO 10464, EO 10560) share an identical, recurring Eisenhower-era drafting convention: subordinating all delegated functions to the Secretary of State's foreign-policy responsibilities. Combined with the earlier late-Carter cluster (EO 12088/12139/12217/12264), this is a second instance of genuinely major policy programs clustering at low structural weight for a shared, nameable reason rather than by coincidence — worth watching for whether this is a recurring feature of *delegation-of-statutory-functions* orders specifically, a category that seems to reliably produce clean scores across multiple administrations because the underlying design pattern (many named agencies, explicit foreign-policy or congressional-authority carve-outs, narrow purpose-bounded reservations) recurs independent of who is president.

**The quiet-regression pattern has a mirror image: quiet strengthening.** EO 10956 (August 1961) amends an existing atomic-energy international-cooperation order to add a presidential referral-and-disapproval step for determinations that previously had none. It's a small, easy-to-overlook amendment, but it demonstrates that the diff-aware coding practice adopted for catching regressions (EO 12072, EO 9296) cuts both ways — the same comparison-against-the-predecessor step that catches a removed safeguard also surfaces an added one. Worth keeping both directions in view rather than only watching for erosion.

**The most structurally significant individual-exemption order in the corpus: EO 11154, exempting J. Edgar Hoover from compulsory retirement "for an indefinite period of time."** Every other individual retirement exemption coded in this project (EO 7525, EO 8047, EO 8231, EO 8287, EO 9033) used the same statutory mechanism but was bounded to a specific six-month or one-year term requiring periodic renewal. This one has no term at all. It scores 35.7% — CRITICAL on Second/Third-Order Effects — because it removes, without any defined end-point, the specific safeguard (mandatory retirement age) designed to ensure turnover in powerful federal positions, applied to the single most consequential law-enforcement office in the government. Hoover went on to serve until his 1972 death, a period historians widely document as including significant abuses of federal surveillance and law-enforcement power. The instrument scores the architecture — an indefinite exemption from a turnover safeguard — rather than adjudicating Hoover's tenure itself, but the historical record is exactly the kind of documented consequence this project's structural framework is meant to anticipate rather than only explain after the fact.

**A direct sequel to an earlier finding, and a clean illustration that brevity isn't good design: EO 11190 revokes and replaces EO 10651.** EO 10651 (1956) was logged earlier for its detailed Ready Reserve screening criteria, including a notable elected-officials carve-out. EO 11190 (December 1964) explicitly revokes it and replaces the entire detailed framework — age/fitness standards, hardship provisions, critical-occupation rules, the periodic reporting requirement — with a single unconditioned sentence delegating all screening-regulation authority to the Secretary of Defense. The successor order is roughly one-tenth the length of its predecessor, and it scores *higher* (35.7% vs. 22.2%). This is the clearest instance yet of the quiet-regression pattern (EO 12072, EO 9296), and the most severe: rather than narrowly weakening one provision, it eliminates an entire criteria-based framework and its accountability mechanism in favor of unconstrained discretion. Worth keeping as a standing counter-example whenever a short order might otherwise read as "at least it's simple" — simple and unconstrained are not the same thing.

**A domestic military deployment order scoring CRITICAL: EO 11403, issued the day after Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination.** In response to the resulting Washington, D.C. riots, this order authorizes the Secretary of Defense to deploy federal military forces and federalize the D.C. National Guard, with National Guard units activated "for an indefinite period... until relieved," and operational authority framed as "all appropriate steps" and "such of the Armed Forces... as he may deem necessary." It scores 33.3%, CRITICAL on Second/Third-Order Effects, reflecting the genuine gravity of indefinite, broadly-discretionary domestic military deployment as a category of governmental action — regardless of the legitimacy of responding to the specific crisis that prompted it. This is a useful marker for how the instrument should treat the most serious category of orders in the corpus: acute emergency response can still be evaluated on its own architectural terms (duration, discretion, reporting) without that evaluation implying anything about whether the underlying response was justified.

**Two established patterns each get a third confirmation in the same batch.** EO 11859 (May 1975) incorporates new Privacy Act of 1974 compliance requirements into a routine tax-inspection order, materially more protective than its 1969 predecessor EO 11465 — a third "quiet strengthening" alongside EO 10956. EO 11890 (December 1975) narrows a 1953 McCarthy-era UN Secretariat loyalty-screening standard to require specific intent and unlawful conduct rather than bare association — a third "power-narrowing reads clean" case alongside EO 11785. Neither is a surprising find on its own, but both patterns are now supported by three independent instances spanning different subject matter and multiple administrations, which is a reasonable threshold for treating them as real, recurring features of how executive orders get drafted and revised over time rather than one-off coincidences.

**A fourth independent confirmation of the Zombie Emergency Trap score, and a gap closed in its timeline.** EO 11940 (September 1976) scores 54.5% — exactly the same as EO 11796 (1974), EO 11810 (1974), and EO 12730 (1990). Its own citation chain (EO 11533 of 1970, continued through EO 11683, EO 11798, EO 11818, and EO 11907) confirms this pathology ran continuously for roughly fifteen years before Congress passed a durable reauthorization in 1985, not as an isolated 1974 episode as the earlier framing might have suggested. Two things worth taking from this together: substantively, the "temporary" emergency-powers substitute for a lapsed statute turned out to be closer to a permanent fixture for a decade and a half; methodologically, four independent codings of structurally near-identical text, spanning sixteen years and three different presidents, producing the identical score each time is a genuine, concrete data point for the instrument's internal consistency — not a claim that needs to be taken on faith, but one visible directly in the coding record.

## Batch 02 — Kennedy (in progress)

**Continuity across the administration transition: EO 10915 touches both of Batch 01's closing consolidations on day four.** EO 10915 (Jan. 24, 1961) amends both EO 10900 (Food for Peace) and EO 10893 (Mutual Security) — the two major consolidation orders that closed out the Eisenhower administration in this stream — to add a coordinating "Director of the Food-for-Peace Program" position, explicitly preserving rather than displacing the existing delegation structure in both. A clean, continuity-respecting first touch by the new administration on inherited machinery, rather than a disruptive reorganization.

**Major finding: EO 10925 (President's Committee on Equal Employment Opportunity, Mar. 6, 1961) closes the "no defined remedy" gap for contractors, but not for unions.** Batch 01's log tracked a recurring structural gap across EO 8802->EO 9808->EO 9981->EO 10479 (Eisenhower's Government Contract Committee): an advisory/reviewing body with no independent enforcement power. EO 10925 abolishes and absorbs EO 10479's Committee, and for the first time gives the resulting body a real, defined sanctions ladder against contractors — cancellation, debarment, DOJ civil/criminal referral, Comptroller General notification, all with a mandatory pre-debarment hearing. That is a genuine structural improvement, textually verifiable. But the identical enforcement-gap pattern persists, unremedied, for labor unions within this very same order: Sections 304-305 give the Committee only "recommendation" and "notification" powers over unions, no debarment-equivalent sanction. One population's gap closes; the other's does not, in the same instrument. This is also the direct organizational predecessor of the modern OFCCP contractor-compliance regime — exactly the evolution anticipated in the Batch 01 log's speculation about this template's future.

**Template reuse, exact chairman-sharing: EO 10929 is a structural sibling of EO 10891 (Batch 01).** Diff-aware comparison against EO 10891's actual coding record (queried directly, not assumed) confirms EO 10929 (Mar. 24, 1961, NY Harbor maritime dispute) reuses EO 10891's chairman, its balanced-nomination structure, its genuine defined self-termination clause, AND its one identified structural gap — the open-ended "such other provisions of law as the President may hereafter specify" fiscal-bypass clause — verbatim. Confirms this exact commission template (chairman-shared, jointly coordinated, identically funded) was used at least twice within five months for related but formally distinct labor disputes, carrying its one flaw forward unchanged both times.

**Contrast case: EO 10921 lacks the defined-sunset discipline of EO 10891/10929.** A three-member flight-engineer-dispute commission established five weeks before EO 10929, EO 10921 has neither a fixed reporting deadline nor a self-termination clause, and its compensation-setting mechanism ("in a manner to be hereafter determined") is doubly open-ended. Same general commission form as EO 10891/10929, considerably less carefully specified — a reminder that this stream's "commission" template is not monolithic; its clean-sunset design has to be checked each time, not assumed.

**Positive design note: EO 10934 (Administrative Conference of the United States, Apr. 13, 1961) has a genuine built-in sunset as originally conceived.** Though ACUS later became a permanent statutory body (1964 Administrative Conference Act, external to this order), the order's own text requires a Final Report by December 31, 1962 evaluating whether further study is needed — a real, defined reassessment point, notably absent from many superficially similar standing advisory bodies elsewhere in this corpus.

**Methodological consistency maintained: EO 10935 (HUAC tax-inspection grant) scored identically to EO 10815 (Batch 01).** Continuing the deliberate "score structurally, not by subject-matter reputation" choice already made for EO 10815 — both congressional tax-inspection grants score 0% regardless of the requesting committee's own contested historical reputation, per the methodology's "direction matters" rule.

**Worth watching: EO 10936's Attorney General exemption discretion (Reports of Identical Bids, Apr. 24, 1961).** A genuinely pro-transparency, anti-collusion order whose only structural gap is Sec. 4's open-ended delegation letting the AG exempt whole categories of procurement from the identical-bid reporting requirement "based upon his experience," with no further textual bound. Worth tracking whether this exemption authority is later used expansively.

**Major finding: EO 10952 reverses the direction of the Batch 1 civil-defense consolidation narrative.** Diff-aware comparison against this stream's own EO 10773 coding (Batch 1, 55.56%, the culmination of a 16-order ODM-authority-accumulation chain) shows EO 10952 (July 20, 1961, at the height of the Berlin Crisis shelter-program buildup) SPLITS the civil-defense authority EO 10773 had consolidated under one Director: operational functions go to the Secretary of Defense, policy/coordination and continuity-of-government planning stay with OCDM, and OCDM retains an explicit "reviewing and coordinating" check over Defense's new operational role. EO 10952 also adds an annual reporting requirement EO 10773 lacked. A genuine structural de-concentration and accountability improvement, apparently driven by the practical urgency of a large new shelter program rather than any stated critique of the prior consolidation. EO 10958 (Aug. 14) cleanly completes this reorganization by delegating the medical/food stockpile functions EO 10952 itself reserved to the President, to HEW and Agriculture respectively.

**Second confirmed instance of the named-individual civil-service bypass: EO 10959 (Mr. Maurice L. Kowal, Aug. 17, 1961).** Directly parallel to EO 10778 (Batch 1) — a single named person exempted from competitive civil-service appointment rules, with no stated reason in the order's own text. Worth continuing to track how often this template recurs across the corpus.

**Diff-aware, mixed finding: EO 10964 partially remedies a Batch 1 Sunset gap while creating a new, narrower exemption architecture.** Checked directly against this stream's own EO 10501 coding (Batch 1, 45%, Flag 6 PRESENT for an "aspirational... to the fullest extent practicable" declassification standard), EO 10964 (Sept. 20, 1961) converts that aspirational standard into a MANDATORY automatic-downgrading/declassification schedule for two of its four new classification-duration categories (Group 3: 12-year intervals; Group 4: 3-year intervals, 12-year automatic declassification) — a real, textually verifiable improvement. But the same amendment creates two new exemption categories (Group 1: categorically excluded; Group 2: agency-head self-exempted on an individual basis) that leave roughly half the classification system outside any automatic mechanism at all. A clean example of a single order simultaneously fixing one flagged gap and opening a new, narrower one — worth remembering that "diff-aware" findings are not always cleanly positive or negative.

**EO 10530 hub order: two more confirmed touches (EO 10960, EO 10970), now seven total.** EO 10960 (Aug. 21) directly edits a paragraph EO 10889 (Batch 1) had itself added ten months earlier; EO 10970 (Oct. 27) adds an eighth-ish discrete function (domestic per-diem rate-setting authority). The hub-order pattern (EO 10759, 10790, 10836, 10852, 10889 in Batch 1; now 10960, 10970 in Batch 2) continues steadily across the administration transition with no sign of the framework being retired or replaced.

**Positive design note: EO 10957's Berlin Crisis Ready Reserve call-up authority is unusually well-bounded for its stakes.** A significant delegation (involuntary reserve mobilization, extension of enlistments) carries a specific numeric cap (250,000), a unit-level presidential-approval proviso, and a fixed one-year sunset (July 1, 1962) — real structural safeguards on a historically consequential power (the August 1961 Berlin Crisis buildup).

**Major finding: EO 10988 (Employee-Management Cooperation in the Federal Service, Jan. 17, 1962) — the single most historically significant order found in this stream so far, with structural mechanics considerably weaker than its reputation.** This is the founding instrument of federal-sector collective employee representation, directly precedent to the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978. Flag 9 CRITICAL for that historical significance alone. But close reading finds real structural gaps: Sec. 3(a) gives agency heads an unreviewable, subjectively-worded veto over whether ANY union may be recognized at all ("influences opposed to basic democratic principles"); every dispute-resolution mechanism the order creates (Sec. 8(b) arbitration, Sec. 11 unit/election determinations) is explicitly advisory only, subject to the same agency head's final approval; and Sec. 16 gives the CIA, NSA, FBI, and any other national-security-adjacent agency a broad, self-determined opt-out from the entire framework. A genuine parallel to the EO 10865/EO 10450 comparison from Batch 1: a landmark order whose headline achievement coexists with management retaining nearly all of the real decision-making leverage by the order's own design. The companion order issued the same day, EO 10987 (Agency Systems for Appeals from Adverse Actions), is more genuinely protective but carries the now-familiar "CIA carve-out" (CIA/NSA/FBI/AEC/TVA categorically excluded), first identified at EO 10805 (Batch 1).

**Diff-aware confirmation: EO 10973 continues the "reserve the broadest disregard-of-law power to the President" pattern through a second consolidation layer.** EO 10973 (Foreign Assistance Act of 1961 administration) supersedes most of EO 10893 (Batch 1) to create the Agency for International Development — USAID's direct institutional predecessor — barely a year after EO 10893's own consolidation. Checked directly against EO 10893's coding record, EO 10973's Sec. 401(d)(4) carries forward, unchanged, the same deliberate reservation to the President of "determining any provisions of law to be disregarded," continuing an unbroken chain from EO 10822 through EO 10893 to EO 10973 — a consistent design choice surviving two full reorganizations in just over a year.

**Positive design note: EO 10974's fiscal-bypass clause omits the recurring open-ended catchall.** The President's Commission on Campaign Costs (EO 10974) bypasses only two specifically named fiscal-control statutes, without the "such other provisions of law as the President may hereafter specify" catchall that recurs throughout this stream's Emergency-Fund-financed labor commissions (EO 10891, 10929, 10946, 10948) — worth noting as evidence the catchall is a template choice, not an unavoidable feature of this funding mechanism.

**Zombie-invocation pattern continues into new subject matter: EO 10976 ties NASA's founding-era urgency to an eleven-year-old Korean War proclamation.** Suspends the Eight-Hour Law for NASA public works, citing Proclamation 2914 (Dec. 1950) as still-live legal predicate for an entirely forward-looking justification (the early space race) — the same pattern tracked since Batch 1, now extended to a brand-new agency in a brand-new policy domain.

**Positive finding: the nine-order "Emergency Preparedness Functions" series (EO 10997-11005, Feb. 16 1962) is a model of ex ante anti-cannibalization design applied across an entire family of orders.** Each order assigns one agency (Interior, Agriculture, Commerce, Labor, HEW, Post Office, FAA, HHFA, ICC) a distinct civil-defense resource-mobilization planning domain, formalizing what had been informal OCDM-Director "Emergency Preparedness Orders" into proper numbered EOs. Every single order in the series (a) explicitly reserves actual plan-implementation authority to the President — these are planning-only delegations, never operational grants; (b) gives the Office of Emergency Planning Director a genuine coordinating check; and (c) cross-references and specifically excludes the domains assigned to every sibling order (EO 10999/Commerce's Sec. 1(c) contains a seven-item exclusion list naming the other agencies by function). Rather than one order avoiding overlap with a general "don't duplicate other agencies" clause, the whole series was evidently drafted as a coordinated package — Flag 10 (Inter-Agency Cannibalization) scored ABSENT/positive across all nine by deliberate cross-drafting, not by accident.

**Major positive finding: EO 11007 (Prescribing Regulations for the Formation and Use of Advisory Committees, Feb. 26 1962) contains one of the strongest Sunset Provisions found anywhere in this stream.** Sec. 8 imposes a MANDATORY DEFAULT two-year expiration on every federal advisory committee not otherwise fixed by statute, renewable only by an affirmative written agency-head determination filed within 60 days of expiration, repeating every two years thereafter — combined with mandatory meeting minutes/transcripts and Sec. 10's annual public-disclosure requirement (with DOJ audit access). This is the direct executive-branch predecessor to the 1972 Federal Advisory Committee Act (FACA) and a genuine model instance of "sunset done well," worth citing as the positive counter-example whenever this stream flags a standing body's missing sunset elsewhere.

**Diff-aware, mixed finding: EO 11010 (Ryukyu Islands amendment, Mar. 19 1962) democratizes the ordinary machinery while leaving the plenary-emergency clause untouched.** Checked directly against this stream's own EO 10713 record (Batch 1, 70%, the stream's highest-scoring order): EO 11010 adds direct popular election of the legislature and a real two-thirds veto-override procedure, but Sec. 11(a)'s "assumption of full authority...for security reasons" clause — the mechanism most responsible for EO 10713's original score — carries forward completely unchanged. A clean example of a reform that improves day-to-day governance while leaving the most consequential emergency-override power untouched. Scored 65%, just under EO 10713's original 70%.

**EO 10530 hub order: eighth confirmed touch (EO 11012).** Adds commuted-rate travel-reimbursement authority and consolidates/revokes ten prior travel-allowance orders dating to 1946 — a genuine housekeeping simplification alongside the hub-order pattern's continued, unremarked persistence (EO 10759, 10790, 10836, 10852, 10889 in Batch 1; EO 10960, 10970, 11012 in Batch 2).

**Contrast finding: EO 11021 (Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands, May 7 1962) is a notably better-designed plenary-territorial-administration order than its Ryukyu Islands sibling (EO 11010, same chunk).** Both delegate full executive/legislative/judicial authority over a Pacific trusteeship population. But EO 11021's Sec. 1 proviso expressly reserves the single most consequential, security-sensitive power — declaring areas closed and determining UN Charter Article 87-88 applicability — to the President personally, rather than leaving it with the territorial administrator as EO 10713/11010 do. State Department consultation and UN-relations channeling are also explicitly preserved. Same delegation "shape," meaningfully different allocation of the one power that matters most.

**Third confirmed instance of the named-individual civil-service bypass: EO 11024 (Dr. Alan T. Waterman, June 4 1962).** Directly parallel to EO 10778 (Batch 1) and EO 10959 (Batch 2, chunk 3) — a single named person (here, NSF's founding Director) exempted from mandatory retirement with no stated rationale in the order's own text, despite a ready justification (continuity of NSF leadership) being available.

**Meta-order finding: EO 11030 (June 19 1962) is the internal procedural framework governing how every other order in this stream gets made.** Requires both Bureau of the Budget and Attorney General sign-off before any Executive order or proclamation reaches the President for signature — a genuine positive accountability design applied to the President's own order-issuance process, with only a narrow, undefined urgency exception for bypassing the Federal Register's pre-signature clerical (not substantive) review.

**Template-recurrence finding: the "utilized...as sparingly as may be practical" self-restraint proviso spreads from EO 11014 to EO 11034 within ten weeks.** EO 11014 (World's Fair, Apr. 17 1962, chunk 4) and EO 11034 (Cultural Exchange Act, June 25 1962, this chunk) both pair a broad (14-15 item) procurement/personnel-law waiver bundle with near-identical self-restraint language directing agencies to use the waivers sparingly and substitute administrative limitations where practicable. EO 11034's Sec. 11(b) also explicitly cross-references EO 11014 ("shall neither limit nor be limited by") — clean evidence of deliberate, coordinated drafting between contemporaneous orders rather than coincidental overlap.

**Positive contrast: EO 11045 (Aug. 21 1962) affirmatively terminates a 21-year-old wartime security designation.** Discontinues the 1941 Guam Naval Defensive Sea Area/Airspace Reservation — a rare instance in this corpus of an order ending rather than perpetuating emergency-era authority, a useful counterpoint to the recurring zombie-invocation pattern (e.g., EO 11037, same chunk, which continues to rest on the twelve-year-old Proclamation 2914).

**Refinement of the zombie-invocation pattern: EO 11037 shows not all Proclamation 2914 reuses are equally attenuated.** EO 11037's gold-licensing amendment invokes Proc. 2914 for Trading with the Enemy Act gold controls — the proclamation's original, on-point subject matter — unlike EO 10976's use of the same proclamation to suspend the Eight-Hour Law for NASA construction (an unrelated policy borrowed under the same standing emergency). Both leave a twelve-year-old emergency unexamined, but one is a legally coherent application and the other an opportunistic one; worth distinguishing "on-point" from "borrowed" zombie invocations going forward.

**Major diff-aware finding: EO 11051 (Sept. 27 1962) re-centralizes the OEP Director's authority to a score matching, almost to the decimal, the original EO 10773 consolidation it explicitly supersedes.** Checked directly against this stream's own EO 10773 coding (Batch 1, 55.56%) and the intervening EO 10952/10958 de-concentration reform (Batch 2, 11.11%/0%), EO 11051 fully re-codifies and broadens the Director's central-decision-making mandate across nearly the entire emergency-preparedness domain (Sec. 203(c) restores "responsibility for making...central decisions in the initial period of an emergency") and lands at 55.56% — the exact same structural-weight percentage as EO 10773 itself, computed independently. Yet the order also explicitly preserves (Sec. 207(a), by direct cross-reference) rather than reverses the EO 10952 operational/policy split between Defense and OEP — meaning the specific accountability improvement identified in that reform survives even as the broader consolidation trend resumes. A genuinely cyclical structural-weight finding: the apex civil-defense authority swings from consolidation (EO 10773) to de-concentration (EO 10952/10958) and back to a numerically identical level of consolidation (EO 11051) within four years, immediately preceding the Cuban Missile Crisis. Also notable: EO 11051 lists "Executive Order No. 9981 of July 26, 1948" among revoked orders — a citation flagged for independent verification given that EO number's well-known historical association with a different subject matter, possibly a source-text transcription irregularity rather than a substantive claim.

**Second confirmed instance of the domestic military-enforcement template: EO 11053 (Mississippi, Sept. 30 1962) reuses the EO 10730 (Little Rock, 1957) design verbatim, including its exact "indefinite period...until relieved" federalization language.** Diff-aware comparison against this stream's own EO 10730 coding (Batch 1, 20%) confirms the same Insurrection Act statutory basis, the same proclamation-then-order structure, the same discretionary "as he may deem necessary/appropriate" standards, and the identical open-ended National Guard federalization phrase. EO 11053 scores identically (20%) to EO 10730 given the structurally identical design — a clean confirmation that this template was reused essentially unchanged for the University of Mississippi desegregation crisis five years after Little Rock.

**Major finding, parallel to the EO 10988 pattern: EO 11063 (Equal Opportunity in Housing, Nov. 20 1962) pairs a well-designed enforcement structure with a narrow, explicitly prospective-only scope.** The order's Committee structure, annual reporting, DOJ-referral channel, and lender-facing sanctions ladder (Sec. 302) are all genuinely well-built. But Sec. 101's core prohibitions apply only to federal housing assistance "hereafter agreed to be made" or "hereafter insured" — leaving the large pre-existing stock of FHA/VA-insured housing entirely outside the order's reach by explicit textual design. As with EO 10988 (federal labor relations, Batch 2 chunk 3), a landmark-reputation order whose real-world bite is considerably narrower than its historical significance would suggest — here by a deliberate scope limitation rather than a weak enforcement mechanism.

**Third confirmed instance of the self-restraint waiver-bundle template: EO 11077 (Migration and Refugee Assistance Act, Jan. 22 1963).** Following EO 11014 (World's Fair) and EO 11034 (Cultural Exchange Act), both Batch 2 chunk 5, EO 11077 pairs an eight-item procurement/personnel-law waiver bundle with the identical "utilized in a prudent manner and as sparingly as may be practical" self-restraint directive — confirming this language had become standard administration-wide boilerplate for broad-waiver delegations rather than a one-off drafting choice in any single order.

**Positive finding: EO 11081 (Jan. 29 1963), a dense procedural rewrite of UCMJ Article 15 nonjudicial-punishment rules, contains genuine due-process safeguards rarely seen elsewhere in this corpus.** A mandatory right to demand trial by court-martial in lieu of nonjudicial punishment (narrower only for vessel-attached personnel, an operationally-explained exception), a right to remain silent with an explicit warning, mandatory legal-officer review of appeals from all but the least serious punishments, a double-punishment bar, and numerically bounded punishment tiers keyed to the imposing officer's rank. A useful positive contrast to this stream's more open-ended discretionary grants — length and density do not always signal structural risk (cf. EO 11009, Batch 2 chunk 4, similarly dense but low-risk).

**Second wave of the Emergency Preparedness Functions series confirmed: fourteen orders total, spanning a full year, complete government-wide coverage.** EO 11087-11095 (Feb. 26, 1963, this chunk) extend the identical template first seen at EO 10997-11005 (Feb. 1962, Batch 2 chunk 4) to State, Treasury, AEC, CAB, Civil Service Commission, FCC, GSA, and two omnibus multi-agency orders (eight financial regulators plus VA; TVA/Railroad Retirement Board/NASA/FPC/NSF). Every instance in both waves preserves the same three positive design features: a President-only "Emergency Actions" implementation clause, a genuine OEP "Presidential coordination" check, and explicit cross-referencing between sibling orders (and to EO 11051, this batch chunk 6) to prevent overlap — a full 14-order family built and maintained with consistent anti-cannibalization discipline across a full year and an intervening major reorganization (EO 11051).

**Diff-aware finding: EO 11114 (June 22 1963) extends EO 10925's contractor nondiscrimination regime to federally-assisted construction, carrying forward its one identified gap unchanged.** Checked directly against this stream's own EO 10925 coding (Batch 2 chunk 1, 40%), EO 11114 genuinely expands coverage from direct government contracts to federally-assisted construction (grants, loans, insurance, guarantees) with the same due-process protections and sanctions ladder — a substantively significant early step toward the 1965 EO 11246 contract-compliance regime. But Sec. 104 carries forward EO 10925's own undefined "special circumstances in the national interest" exemption standard essentially unchanged, confirming that a structural gap identified early in a regulatory regime's life can persist through subsequent genuine expansions of that regime's scope.

**Third and fourth confirmed instances of the domestic military-enforcement template: EO 11111 and EO 11118 (Alabama, June and September 1963).** Following EO 10730 (Little Rock, 1957) and EO 11053 (Mississippi, 1962, Batch 2 chunk 6), these two Alabama orders (the "stand in the schoolhouse door" incident and a subsequent school-desegregation crisis) reuse the identical template — including the exact "indefinite period...until relieved" federalization phrase — and score identically (20%) to both predecessors. Across four instances spanning 1957-1963, the template shows no drift toward either stronger or weaker structural safeguards; it was a stable, reusable design throughout the civil rights era. EO 11118's own text cites Title 10 sections "382, 383 and 384" as being within "Chapter 15" (which actually comprises sections 331-335) — flagged as a likely source-text transcription irregularity (probably for 332-334, used in every other instance) rather than treated as a substantive finding.
